# The Idea

Let's design a world / structure / story outline / game plan for a Unity project. This game should be a tactical rpg combat game like XCOM 2 and Fire Emblem.

---

- 4 factions are vying for control over the (fictional, not Sol) solar system. 
- Each faction has a strength and a moral, fatal flaw, where there is no perfect ending regardless of which side wins in the end. Something must be sacrificed, and the decisions of the player will determine what that sacrifice is.
- The player is in a position like the fictional group XCOM, where they are a neutral party that has the power to influence decisions and outcomes via the game's combat scenarios, where a variety of objectives may be present on each map, and completing any number of them results in different levels of success, but where going for the perfect 100% is nearly impossible and will result in permanent losses of named characters in their squad that are deployed.
- Over time, the conflicts the player chooses to get involved in for one faction or another will begin to align them over time and provide support for the given entity. Other factions may form a coalition to fight against the rising power and success of the player supported faction, escalating tension and moral problems and scenarios the player will have to face in each combat scenario.
- In the event of complete failure on a map or trying to remain neutral, the game reaches an end scenario where mass extinction events occur as the player was unable to influence, de-escalate, or hinder rising problems and development in warring factions. 
- Winning the war for a given faction gives the results of "it's over, but at what cost". The player should see hints at the effects of their choices throughout the game without it being thrown in their faces, but should see the full consequences of their choices by the end.
- Each decision should be a difficult moral dilemma where they have to choose between the life, liberty, freedom, success, and happiness of humanity as a whole, as well as weighing the cost of being efficient with their resources (supplies, characters, ethically challenging technology research, accepting help from questionable third parties (space pirates?), mechanizing, ai management and government, escalating tactics (if you start doing mass destruction, the other side may become more willing to also escalate))

## Worldbuilding
- The concept of super weapons (something like Star Wars Empire's Star Destroyers) is a novelty and having control of the few vessels of this size is a terrible force to be reckoned with. 
- The player will be involved in guerilla squad combats, but will have the option of launching to assault supply ships and larger vessels in orbit if the circumstances allow (like XCOM: Enemy Within's alien supply ship raid)
- The reality of the world will be somewhat of a low-fantasy, high-tech environment, where space travel and the like exists, as well as the potential for Psionics and energy weapons. No alien species, only Humans. (Aliens may be hinted at, but are still widely a myth. Maybe if game is successful we explore a DLC or Game 2 where aliens get involved)
- Influences: Red Rising, Hunger Games, Star Wars, XCOM, Fire Emblem

## Game Wish List Wow Factors
- The concept where you are starting a tactical operation on the ground, but have the opportunity to send a squaddie or two up into orbit to do a hit and run inside an orbital station or vessel and destroy it should be fun
- Fun abilities that can change the course of a battle
- Battle difficulty should be intense, but possible. Sending a squad in to "level them up" should be punished as not bringing in enough firepower.
- Allow for different tech trees based on which groups the player is supporting.

## End Goal of the Story as the developer
- I want the player to struggle with the moral decisions that they make for the sake of efficiency. There should be no "best ending", but rather different endings that make the player question if the decisions they made were the right ones and if they are ok with what they did for the sake of winning.

# Game Plan 1
1. The World

Setting:

The game takes place in the Erebus System, a solar system beyond Earth.

Populated planets, moons, and orbital stations act as contested areas. Some planets are resource-rich, some strategically located, some culturally or politically significant.

Low-fantasy, high-tech: technology is advanced (energy weapons, mech suits, orbital railguns, limited AI), but humanity is still fallible, and morals/factions drive the story.

Psionics exist but are rare and controversial.

No aliens, only human factions; aliens are myths and rumors that influence faction propaganda and paranoia.

Gameplay Integration:

Each planet/moon is a node on a strategic map (Overworld) that the player can influence via missions.

Space stations, orbital vessels, and supply ships can be mission objectives. Some missions may allow transitioning from ground combat to orbital boarding (dynamic, wow factor).

2. Factions (4)

Each faction has a strength and a fatal flaw. The player’s support influences their power, alliances, and moral consequences.

Faction	Strength	Fatal Flaw	Playable Player Themes
The Solar Dominion	Highly organized military, strong fleet, advanced tech	Authoritarian, sacrifices freedom for security	Efficiency vs. ethics, controlling power vs. humanity
The Free Colonies	Democratic, morally high ground, tech adaptability	Politically fractious, slow to act, vulnerable	Protecting civilians, slow to act, may lose ground
The Mercantile Syndicate	Wealth, logistics, espionage, pirates	Corruption, greed, willing to exploit civilians	Gain material advantage, face moral compromise
The Purity Covenant	Religious/ideological unity, zeal, psionics	Dogmatic, intolerant, can commit atrocities in the name of faith	Morality vs. fanatical order, consequences of zeal

Gameplay Implications:

Supporting a faction opens tech trees, abilities, and mission types.

Over-supporting one faction triggers coalitions from others.

Each faction provides morally gray options: “help now, consequences later” style decisions.

3. Player Role

Neutral organization similar to XCOM.

Overworld Layer: Choose which conflicts to intervene in, allocate resources, manage squad, develop tech, and research controversial projects.

Tactical Layer: Combat missions on the ground, orbital boarding, sabotage, rescue operations, intelligence gathering.

Moral Layer: Decisions aren’t clear-cut; efficiency often costs lives, loyalty, or ethics.

Player Constraints & Tension Mechanics:

Squad size and fatigue.

Supply limits and ammo scarcity.

Optional objectives give higher rewards but increase risk of permanent losses.

Mission failure or inaction has long-term consequences (faction strength grows unchecked, mass casualties).

4. Tactical Gameplay Design

Combat Type: Turn-based, grid, XCOM/Fire Emblem hybrid.

Squads: 4–6 units for ground, optional boarding of ships/stations with 1–2 units.

Objectives: Multiple layers (primary, secondary, secret). Rewards vary depending on completion.

Dynamic Events:

Civilians at risk → must decide to save or push forward.

Enemy reinforcements → delay could cause stronger forces to arrive.

Moral dilemmas mid-mission (capture vs. kill, destroy supplies vs. preserve).

Abilities: Unique squad abilities, faction-dependent tech, psionics for special tactics.

Difficulty: Hard but fair. Efficient tactics are rewarded; recklessness is punished.

5. Tech Trees & Resources

Tech trees vary by faction supported.

Solar Dominion: Heavy armor, orbital railguns, drones.

Free Colonies: Adaptive tech, shields, morale bonuses.

Mercantile Syndicate: Explosives, hacking, sabotage.

Purity Covenant: Psionics, energy weapons, zeal-fueled boosts.

Research choices affect combat options and moral dilemmas (AI weapons, chemical/bioweapons, psionic enhancement).

Supplies are finite and must be allocated carefully between missions.

6. Story Arcs & Progression

Introduction: Player organization is small and neutral. Introduces mechanics and factions.

Rising Tension: Factions clash over key planets/resources. Player picks missions to support one faction or mitigate conflict.

Faction Alignment: Player begins leaning toward one faction; opposing factions form coalitions. Missions escalate in difficulty and stakes.

Endgame:

Complete domination by a faction → final missions unlock.

Mass casualty consequences if neutrality or failure.

Player sees outcomes of moral compromises, loyalty decisions, and efficiency-focused choices.

Key Story Mechanic:

Every mission can have permanent consequences for characters, faction power, and civilian populations.

7. Wow Factor / Unique Selling Points

Ground → Orbital Combat Transitions: Send a squad into orbit mid-battle.

Moral & Strategic Layer Integration: Decisions on overworld directly affect tactical battles and vice versa.

Faction-Dependent Tech Trees: Deep replayability.

Hard, Rewarding Combat: Tactical depth, rewarding risk-taking, punishing recklessness.

No Perfect Ending: Each ending is bittersweet or morally challenging, pushing reflection on choices.

8. Early Unity Development Plan

Phase 1: Overworld / Strategic Layer

Map with planets, orbital stations, supply lines, faction influence mechanics.

Phase 2: Faction Integration

Tech trees, moral dilemmas, branching missions.

Phase 3: Dynamic Story & Consequence Engine

Permanent squad losses, moral feedback, ending variations.

Phase 4: Polish & “Wow Factor”

Orbital raids, cinematic mission sequences, faction-specific abilities.

Phase 5: Tactical Combat Core

Grid-based movement, cover, basic combat mechanics, unit stats, simple AI.

The player isn’t a neutral XCOM-style agent; they are the hidden power behind all factions, controlling resources, research, and strategic escalation.

Each faction believes it is acting independently, but the player can subtly or overtly manipulate decisions, technology access, and mission outcomes.

The goal isn’t “winning for a faction,” it’s achieving your own long-term objectives, which are morally ambiguous.

Narrative/Moral Implications:

Moral Grayness is Amplified: Every decision the player makes impacts innocent civilians, named characters, and even entire factions, but they never get the moral “cover” of a faction ideology.

Player Choices = Systemic Consequences: Choosing efficiency, ethically gray tech, or controversial funding doesn’t just escalate one faction; it escalates the entire system.

Hidden vs. Visible Consequences:

Early game: player manipulations are subtle → factions appear autonomous.

Mid-to-late game: the player’s fingerprints become clear → factions may rebel, missions get harder, AI escalates against you if your manipulations are discovered.

No Perfect Ending: Even if the player achieves total dominance, the cost is societal collapse, human rights violations, or irreversible moral corruption.

Gameplay Implications
1. Faction Manipulation Layer

Player can allocate support, resources, or research to factions:

Example: Fund Authoritarian research → they gain orbital AI drones.

Example: Support Populist propaganda → chaos spreads, creating opportunities or threats elsewhere.

Player sees the direct tactical and strategic consequences of these manipulations on the battlefield.

2. Mission Design Layer

Player chooses which factions perform missions, indirectly affecting outcomes:

You can choose a faction to attack a location → collateral damage is higher if you push them to extreme measures.

Optional objectives can be manipulated to favor efficiency or moral high ground → player decides the tradeoff.

3. Ethics vs Efficiency (Amplified)

Every morally gray or ruthless choice increases AI aggressiveness, escalation, and faction ruthlessness, just as before.

Moral “good choices” (protecting civilians, avoiding extreme tech) put the player at a systemic disadvantage, because factions under your control may refuse to act efficiently, allowing others to gain power.

4. Visibility & Risk

The player must balance secrecy and influence:

If factions detect your manipulations → missions harder, loyalty of units drops, factions may turn against your plans.

The more overt your control → higher efficiency, but moral consequences escalate.

Narrative/Story Opportunities

Player sees the unintended consequences of their manipulations.

Factions may begin to act independently against your plans, creating emergent conflict.

Moral reflection is deeper because the player cannot claim innocence: they are the root cause of the civil war’s suffering.

Endgame is inevitably bittersweet: either total dominance with human cost, partial failure, or chaos spiraling out of control.

Requires clear feedback on manipulation effects so the player understands cause-and-effect.

Unique Selling Point

Most tactical RPGs follow “good vs evil.” A morally gray, democratic civil war with consequences for both ethical and ruthless play is very rare and memorable.

Cons / Challenges

Complexity

Lots of moving parts: faction loyalty, moral meters, AI adaptation, branching outcomes.

May be harder to communicate to players compared to “defeat the bad guys” gameplay.

Player Frustration Risk

Some players may feel punished for doing the “right thing” (higher difficulty, delayed progression).

Others may feel guilty for efficiency/ruthlessness → could reduce enjoyment if not balanced carefully.

Implementation Load

Requires robust systems in Unity: AI adaptation, branching narrative, unit memory, dynamic difficulty.

Pros of Classic “Good vs Evil” Approach

Simplicity & Clarity

Player understands their role immediately: save the innocent, defeat the villains.

Clear objectives and feedback → easier to make accessible and fun.

Lower Development Complexity

AI doesn’t need to track moral metrics; branching consequences can be lighter.

Easier to design missions that feel rewarding without moral balancing.

Immediate Gratification

Players can feel “good” about doing the right thing without constant moral tension.

Easier to design moments of triumph.

Middle Ground Approach (Recommended)

You don’t have to go fully “good vs evil” to make a fun game — you can:

Keep Ethical Choices Optional / Layered

Core gameplay is classic tactical combat with clear objectives.

Optional missions or secondary objectives carry moral weight, but the player isn’t punished for ignoring them.

Use Moral Choices as Flavor / Strategic Depth

Player can play efficiently with minimal moral penalties if they choose.

Moral consequences become story flavor and emergent tension, not mandatory stress.

Balance Immediate Fun with Moral Complexity

Ensure that the core tactical gameplay is satisfying, while moral dilemmas enhance replayability and narrative depth.

Bottom Line

Don’t force the moral tension into every single mechanic.

Let player choice dictate how much moral stress they experience.

Keep core tactical gameplay satisfying and rewarding, independent of moral outcomes.

Generic Resource / Supply Missions

These are optional or semi-random missions that occur between story-critical missions. They help the player acquire resources, squad upgrades, and equipment, but often come with minor tactical or moral decisions.

Purpose

Resource Management

Supplies, weapons, medkits, armor, orbital intel, tech parts.

Squad Progression

Experience for unnamed units, leveling, and optional skill unlocks.

Tactical Decisions

Risk vs reward: gain more supplies at cost of squad stress, casualties, or moral compromises.

Optional Moral Dilemmas

Some supply missions can include civilians, hostile occupiers, or ethical consequences.

Implementation Notes

Mission Frequency: Optional missions appear between story missions, letting the player plan squad upgrades or gather extra resources.

Resource Reward Scaling:

Act 1: small amounts, encourage careful choice.

Act 2: larger rewards, may allow earlier use of precursor tech.

Act 3: high-value rewards, optional to make late-game more manageable if player struggled.

Moral Tension Integration: Even minor supply missions can affect:

Civilian casualty counts

Squad loyalty / morale

Faction perception (trust / suspicion)

Randomized / Dynamic Objectives: To increase replayability, some generic missions can appear with randomized enemy placement, optional secondary objectives, or timed elements.

Integration Into Three-Act Pipeline

Act 1 – Small-scale, mostly stealth or defensive supply missions.

Act 2 – Larger-scale resource missions; moral and efficiency trade-offs become more meaningful.

Act 3 – Critical resources, experimental prototypes, or tech recovery missions directly feed into final endgame operations.

✅ Key Takeaways

Supply missions provide tactical flexibility and prevent the player from feeling static or overpowered early.

Even minor resource operations reinforce moral tension when choices affect civilians or environment.

They integrate naturally between story-critical missions without breaking narrative pacing.

Adds XCOM-style “campaign grind” and risk-reward planning while keeping focus on the moral and extinction-tech story arc.
